Turtle Categories

Following Are The Five Types Of Turtle Species Which Are In Sri-Lanka

GREEN SEA TURTLE

One of the most widely dispersed sea turtle species is the green sea turtle (Chelonia mydas), which may be found in tropical and subtropical oceans all around the world, including Sri Lanka. What you should know about green sea turtles is as follows: Green sea turtles have a streamlined body with a dorsoventrally flattened carapace (shell), which can grow up to 4 feet (120 cm) in length and weigh on average between 300 and 400 pounds (136 to 181 kg). Their name derives from the form of their carapace, which is normally brown to black in color with hints of green, yellow, and olive. Small, overlapping scutes cover the carapace. Green sea turtles have a different diet than other sea turtles and are largely herbivorous in their feeding habits.

HAWKSBILL SEA TURTLE

The severely endangered hawksbill sea turtle (Eretmochelys imbricata) is renowned for its distinctive look and ecological significance. The following are some significant hawksbill sea turtle facts: Hawksbill turtles stand out thanks to their tiny, pointed beak-like mouth and exquisitely patterned carapace (shell). Scutes, the overlapping scales that make up the carapace, have an eye-catching tortoise-shell pattern. The turtles' bodies can move and flex readily thanks to the overlapping nature of their brown, amber, and black scutes. Size: In comparison to other sea turtle species, hawksbill sea turtles are quite little. They normally weigh between 100 and 150 pounds (45 to 60 kg) and reach lengths of about 2.5 to 3 feet (75 to 90 cm).

OLIVE RIDLEY SEA TURTLE

Olive Ridley sea turtles, also known as Lepidochelys olivacea, are small sea turtles found in warm waters. They have a synchronized nesting behavior called "arribadas" and feed on jellyfish, shrimp, crabs, snails, and algae. They are listed as "vulnerable" on the IUCN Red List of Threatened Species, facing threats like habitat loss, poaching, fishing gear capture, and pollution. Females reach sexual maturity at 10-15 years old and lay eggs multiple times during a single season. Efforts are ongoing to protect nesting sites, minimize human-induced threats, and ensure long-term survival for these fascinating creatures.

LEATHERBACK SEA TURTLE

Dermochelys coriacea, the leatherback sea turtle, is the largest species and has a unique carapace with ridges and a black or dark gray color. They have a global distribution in tropical and temperate oceans, migrating thousands of miles between nesting and feeding grounds. They primarily eat jellyfish and have powerful jaws and esophageal papillae in their throat. Leatherbacks lay multiple clutches of eggs on tropical and subtropical sandy beaches. They are listed as "vulnerable" on the IUCN Red List of Threatened Species and face threats like fishing gear capture, habitat degradation, pollution, and marine debris ingestion. Conservation efforts focus on protecting nesting sites, fishing gear modifications, and raising awareness about their importance.

LEGGERHEAD SEA TURTLE

Leggerhead sea turtles, also known as Caretta caretta, are a large, carnivorous marine species found in the Atlantic, Pacific, and Indian Oceans. They have a wide distribution and primarily feed on marine invertebrates. They nest during nighttime, laying clutches of 100 to 120 eggs. They are listed as "endangered" on the IUCN Red List of Threatened Species, facing threats like habitat destruction, coastal development, pollution, fisheries bycatch, and climate change. Conservation efforts focus on protecting nesting beaches, fishing gear modifications, and reducing human impacts.
